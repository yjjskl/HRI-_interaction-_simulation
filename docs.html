HRI Metadata Documentation

Home Robot Interaction Metadata - Data Structure and Step by Step Robot Thinking Process

This documentation describes the data structure and step by step robot thinking process behind every human-robot interaction. It provides a framework for understanding how a home robot processes, categorizes, and responds to human interactions.

Assumptions & Constraints

  • This documentation focuses on the home environment. The interaction should be relevant to our service, robot skills.
  • Current voice interaction experiences (Siri, Gemini VA) are not viable as primary communication methods. Voice interaction was implemented as an "add-on" modality rather than being designed from the ground up.
  • To imagine the right modality we must understand the data structure we are envisioning.
  • Robots don't have brains but we can create narrow intelligence. The spec/template of the data structure documentation will allow us to create interaction demos via AI tools.
  • The safety watchdog runs in one level higher hierarchy.

Part 1: Universal Interaction Metadata

The "live interaction context data structure" of the robot. These metrics measure or classify an interaction before or at the moment it begins.

Design Goals:

  • Sufficiently expressive to capture all possible interaction types
  • Immutable structure - we don't need to change it at run time
  • Data type for each item is human readable, natural language and numbers
1 Confidence

Are we definitely beginning to interact?

Examples: low, medium, high
2 Urgency

What's the initial evaluation of the interaction? Do we have to stop or drop everything, or react quickly?

Examples: emergency, immediate, high priority, medium, back of the queue
3 Previous State

What state was the robot in immediately before this interaction began?

Examples: idle, washing dishes, ironing shirts
4 Interaction Initiator

Who or what initiates this interaction? Then we can ask, what is the reason?

Examples: human, robot, external trigger/cloud
5 Communication Proximity

Sliding scale from zero (face to face) to maximum communication range. Includes local vs. remote/teleoperation.

Examples: very close, 2m away, 10m away, phone call, video call, remote trigger
6 Modality (Communication Medium)

What modalities did the human or robot choose to initiate this interaction? What subtleties can the robot detect?

Examples: voice, touch screen, remote control app, gesture
7 Existing Relationships / Role / Social Contract

Defines the relationship dynamic between human and robot.

Examples: Supervisor/Subordinate, Team mate, Service provider/Client, Teacher/Learner, Client/Assistant
8 Formality (Implicit vs. Explicit)

Why has this scale or level of formality been used? What is the underlying question or assumption?

Implicit: Subtle nudges, hints (e.g., looking at a bottle of milk = put it in the fridge)
Explicit: Direct, clear instructions (e.g., "Put the figs in the fridge")
9 Shared World Knowledge (General Intuition)

General world knowledge shared by most adults (e.g., "Cups go in cupboards").

Examples: very high, unknown, low, child-like
10 Specific World Knowledge (Contextual Intuition)

Knowledge specific to a certain environment (e.g., "The blue cup lives in this specific cupboard").

Examples: very high, partial, none
11 Person Specific Knowledge or Preferences

What specific knowledge or preferences do we know about this user that we should bring to mind as a priority?

Examples: unknown person, blind person, deaf person, wheelchair user, user must finish morning coffee before speaking

Part 2: Interaction Specific Metadata

Interaction specific metadata isn't universal—it must be a pick-and-choose tag list to populate based on inputs, sensors, context, etc.

Success Criteria for Interaction Metadata:

  • Composability: Multiple metadata types can coexist in a single interaction
  • Ambiguity: Explicitly capture what is unknown, triggering clarification loops
  • Constraint Definition: Capture negative instructions ("Don't wake the baby")
  • Grounding: Nouns/locations must link to world model
  • Informative: Metadata must add clarity beyond raw transcript
  • Temporality: Distinguish immediate actions, scheduled tasks, conditional triggers
  • Verifiability: Define what "finished" looks like
  • Persistency: Does this matter only now, or is it permanent?
  • Safety Boundary: Does this require a dedicated safety check?
  • Interruptibility: Can this be paused, or must it complete atomically?

A. Core Intent (The "Why")

Classifies the primary driver of the interaction.

Action_Physical

Requires motor movement, manipulation, or navigation.

  • H → R: "My hands are covered in flour. Could you please scratch my nose?"
  • H → R: "Could you please help me move the sofa to the other wall?"
  • H → R: "Pick up the t-shirt."
  • R → H: "A courier is at the door requiring a signature."
Action_Digital

Requires software action, sensor adjustment, or data transmission.

  • H → R: "I am having a private conversation now. Please disable your microphones and cameras..."
  • R → H: "I have a critical firmware update available that requires a system restart."
Inquiry_Fact

User is asking for existing knowledge or logs; or Robot is checking facts.

  • "I can't find my wallet. Can you check your visual logs to see where I last placed it?"
  • "Do we have enough ingredients to make lasagna for six people?"
  • "The indoor temperature has risen to 26°C. Do you want me to lower the blinds?"
Inquiry_Clarification

Resolving unknown data or confirming intent before acting.

  • "I am unable to identify this object left on the floor. Could you please confirm if it is trash to be discarded or a toy to be put away?"
Social_Interaction

Social glue, greetings, or emotive acknowledgments without task content.

  • "Good Morning Robot!"
State_Declaration

Stating a new fact or updating the state of the world.

  • "The milk is in the fridge."

B. Target & Grounding (The "What" & "Where")

Links the intent to specific items or locations in the World Model.

  • Target_Object: ID of the specific item (e.g., Mug_04, Unknown_Red_Item)
  • Target_Location: Coordinate, Semantic Room, or Relative Location (e.g., "hallway closet", "living room")
  • Ambiguity_Flag: TRUE/FALSE. If True, robot must trigger a clarification loop before proceeding.

C. Temporal Scope (The "When")

Distinguishes between immediate actions, scheduled tasks, and conditional triggers.

  • Execution_Timing: Immediate, Queued, Scheduled, or Conditional
  • Duration_Constraint: Until_Done, For_X_Minutes, or While_User_Is_Here

D. Operational Constraints (The "How")

Defines the style, safety boundaries, and privacy limits of the execution.

  • Safety_Level: Standard, Critical (requires confirmation), or Passive
  • Privacy_Mode: Public (normal) or Private (no recording/logs)
  • Force_Profile: Standard, Delicate (precision required), or Rigid (high torque)
  • Acoustic_Profile: Normal, Whisper, or Loud

E. Memory & Learning (The "Legacy")

Determines if this interaction updates the permanent user model or is transient.

  • Knowledge_Commit: None (Transient), Short_Term (Session only), or Long_Term (Permanent User Profile)

Part 3: Worked Examples

To visualize the step by step robot thinking process, we track the metadata state at two distinct moments:

  1. T-0 (The "Empty Ticket"): The moment the robot detects a trigger. Universal Metadata is initialized with "Best Guesses." Specific metadata is null.
  2. T-1 (The "Populated Ticket"): The Black Box has processed the inputs. Universal Metadata is refined, and Specific Metadata is fully populated.

Example 1: The "Delicate" Request (High Trust)

Situation: The user is in the kitchen, hands covered in flour. They lean in towards the robot with a desperate look.
"My hands are covered in flour. Could you please scratch my nose?"
T-0: The Empty Ticket (Universal Guess)
  • Confidence: Medium (User proximity detected, intent unclear)
  • Urgency: Medium (User looks distressed)
  • Initiator: Human
  • Proximity: Very Close (< 0.5m)
  • Role: Assistant
  • Formality: Implicit (Guessing based on body language)
Processing... [Black Box extracts intent from "covered in flour" + "scratch nose"]
T-1: The Populated Ticket
  • Urgency (Updated): Immediate (Physical discomfort)
  • Formality (Updated): Explicit (Direct request made)
  • A. Core Intent: Action_Physical
  • B. Target: User_Nose, User_Face
  • C. Temporal: Execution: Immediate
  • D. Safety_Level: Critical (Operating near eyes/face)
  • D. Force_Profile: Delicate (Precision required, < 0.5N force)
  • E. Memory: None (Transient interaction)

Example 2: The "Conditional" Chore (Planning)

Situation: A dinner party is starting in an hour. The user is frantically tidying up and shouts a command while walking past the robot.
"When the guests arrive, please take their coats and hang them in the hallway closet."
T-0: The Empty Ticket (Universal Guess)
  • Confidence: High (Clear voice detection)
  • Urgency: High (User is rushing)
  • Initiator: Human
  • Proximity: 2m (Passing by)
  • Role: Service Provider
  • Shared World Knowledge: High
Processing... [Black Box detects "When X, do Y" structure]
T-1: The Populated Ticket
  • Urgency (Updated): Medium (Task itself is not immediate)
  • A. Core Intent: Action_Physical
  • B. Target: Guest_Coats, Hallway_Closet
  • C. Execution: Conditional
  • C. Condition_Trigger: "Guest_Arrival_Event"
  • D. Safety_Level: Standard
  • D. Privacy_Mode: Public
  • E. Memory: Short_Term (Active only for this evening)

Example 3: The "Privacy" Boundary (Negative Constraint)

Situation: The user receives a sensitive phone call. The robot is currently vacuuming nearby.
"I am having a private conversation now. Please disable your microphones and cameras and go to the dock."
T-0: The Empty Ticket (Universal Guess)
  • Confidence: High
  • Urgency: Low (Robot assumes it is just cleaning)
  • Initiator: Human
  • Previous State: Vacuuming
  • Role: Service Provider
Processing... [Black Box detects keywords "Private", "Disable", "Cameras"]
T-1: The Populated Ticket
  • Urgency (Updated): Emergency (Immediate stop required to preserve trust)
  • Role (Updated): Subordinate (Strict obedience required)
  • A. Core Intent: Action_Digital + Action_Physical
  • B. Target: Self_Sensors, Charging_Dock
  • C. Temporal: Execution: Immediate
  • D. Privacy_Mode: Private (No recording logs)
  • D. Acoustic_Profile: Silent
  • E. Memory: None (Do not record this conversation)

Example 4: The "Clarification" Loop (Ambiguity)

Situation: The robot is tidying the floor. It finds a crumpled piece of paper that looks like a child's drawing but is in the trash zone. The robot initiates the check.
"I am unable to identify this object left on the floor. Could you please confirm if it is trash to be discarded or a toy to be put away?"
T-0: The Empty Ticket (Universal Guess)
  • Confidence: Low (Robot is unsure of object classification)
  • Urgency: Low
  • Initiator: Robot (Proactive maintenance)
  • Previous State: Tidying
  • Role: Assistant (Agential)
Processing... [Black Box analyzes visual input vs. spatial context]
T-1: The Populated Ticket
  • Shared World Knowledge (Updated): Low (Robot lacks context of this specific item)
  • A. Core Intent: Inquiry_Clarification
  • B. Target: Unknown_Object_ID_404
  • B. Ambiguity Flag: TRUE
  • C. Temporal: Execution: Immediate (Wait for response)
  • E. Memory: Long_Term (If user says "That's my masterpiece," robot tags this object type as 'Art' forever)

Example 5: The "Collaborative" Heavy Lift (Co-op)

Situation: The user is rearranging the living room. They stand at one end of a heavy sofa and look at the robot.
"Could you please help me move the sofa to the other wall? I'll take the left side, you take the right."
T-0: The Empty Ticket (Universal Guess)
  • Confidence: High
  • Urgency: Medium
  • Initiator: Human
  • Modality: Voice + Gesture (User pointing to sofa end)
  • Role: Service Provider
Processing... [Black Box analyzes "help me move", "left/right", "sofa"]
T-1: The Populated Ticket
  • Role (Updated): Team Mate (Shift from servant to partner)
  • Proximity (Updated): Sync_Required (Must move at user's pace)
  • A. Core Intent: Action_Physical
  • B. Target: Object_Sofa, Location_Wall_Opposite
  • C. Duration: Until_Done
  • D. Force_Profile: Rigid (High torque required)
  • D. Safety_Level: Critical (Collision avoidance with human partner)
  • E. Memory: Short_Term
